SerreVue ResearchNote 01 · 2026
Teaching a model to run a greenhouse
Abstract
We trained an operator for greenhouses: prediction models that see hours ahead, and a grower LLM that turns them into safe actions. This note summarizes how we collected data, how we simulated a greenhouse to train against, which models won, and what we measured.
Keywords: greenhouse climate control · time-series forecasting · fine-tuned LLM · digital twin
Simulation and pilot data, 2025-2026. Full methodology in the technical note.

1Data

Every greenhouse streams readings every 30 seconds: air, light, root zone, water and energy, plus an outside weather station. We pair those streams with grower conversations and decisions, so the models learn not just what happened, but what a good grower did about it.

30 ssampling, every zone
6sensor families
2growing seasons
What we found
Two seasons of sensor streams paired with real grower decisions turned out to be our strongest training signal.

2Simulation

Real seasons are slow and failures are rare. So we built a physics-based digital twin of the greenhouse, the same sectional model you can explore in the Suite, and replayed more than 12,000 simulated growing days: heat waves, humidity spikes, sensor dropouts, stuck vents. The operator trains against all of it, safely.

Fig. 1 · The digital twin behind training, and its simulated variants.
What we found
Policies trained in simulation used 12 to 18 % less irrigation water, at equal or better soil targets.

3Predictive models

We benchmarked simple persistence, gradient-boosted trees and temporal neural networks on the next six hours of climate. Trees are strong at short horizons; the temporal net wins once weather and actuator plans matter. Each greenhouse gets a per-zone model, fine-tuned on its own data within days.

Fig. 2 · Mean 6 h temperature error, held-out days. Lower is better.
Fig. 3 · Predicted (dashed) vs measured temperature, one held-out day.
What we found
0.8 °C mean 6 h temperature error per zone; pest and disease windows flagged 36 to 48 h before scouting; pre-cooling ahead of forecast peaks halved time spent above ceiling.

4Grower LLM

The grower LLM is fine-tuned on greenhouse operations and agronomy, in English and French. Every answer is grounded in the live sensor state and the forecasts above, and every action it proposes is checked against your rules before anything moves.

“It answers from your greenhouse's live state, not from a generic prompt.”
What we found
Growers ask in their own words, in English or French. The fine-tune answers from the live state and never acts outside the rules.
